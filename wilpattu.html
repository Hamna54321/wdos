WILPATTU NATIONAL PARK

Sri Lanka has a national park called Wilpattu National Park. The presence of "Willus" (natural lakes), which are sand-rimmed water basins or depressions that naturally fill with rains, is what makes this park special. Situated in Sri Lanka's northwest coast lowland dry zone, the park is roughly 180 km (110 mi) north of Colombo, 30 km (19 km) west of Anuradhapura, and 26 km (16 mi) north of Puttalam. The park spans between 0–152 m (0–499 ft) above sea level and is 1,317 km2 (508 sq mi) (131,693 hectares) in size. There are roughly 106 lakes (Willu) and tanks dispersed across Wilpattu. The biggest and most established national park in Sri Lanka is Wilpattu The biggest and most established national park in Sri Lanka is Wilpattu. The population of leopards (Panthera pardus kotiya) in Wilpattu is well known worldwide. The Wilderness and Wildlife Conservation Trust conducted a remote camera survey in Wilpattu from July to October 2015. The survey area included photographs of 49 individual leopards, with a core area density between that of Yala National Park's Block I and Horton Plains National Park. Due to security concerns surrounding the Sri Lankan Civil War, the park was closed from December 1988 to March 16, 2003, and it was restored to visitors sixteen years later. Only about 25% of the park is currently open to visitors; the remaining area is mostly covered in scrub or deep forest. The months of February through October are the busiest for visitors, while several private ecotourism companies do safaris all year round. The yearly temperature is approximately 27.2 °C (81.0 °F), while the annual rainfall is approximately 1,000 mm (39 in). The primary sources of rainfall are the northeast monsoon (December to February) and the intermonsoonal showers in March.

Flora and fauna:

Wilpattu is home to a diverse range of vegetation types, including salt grass and low scrub monsoon forest with tall emergents like salamanders (Manilkara hexandra), milla (Vitex altissima), weera (Drypetes sepiaria), ebony (Diospyros ebenum), and wewarna (Alseodaphne semecapriflolia). There are 31 known species of animals in Wilpattu National Park. The following mammals are found in the Wilpattu National Park and are classified as vulnerable species: water buffalo (Bubalus bubalis), sloth bears (Melursus ursinus inornatus), leopards (Panthera pardus kotiya), and elephants (Elephas maximus maximus). Other inhabitants of Wilpattu include the sambar (Rusa unicolor unicolor), spotted deer (Axis axis ceylonensis), mongoose, mouse, and shrew.


Birds:

Found in Wilpattu National Park are the painted stork, open bill, small cormorant, and Sri Lankan junglefowl (Gallus lafayetii), in addition to other species of owls, terns, gulls, eagles, kites, and buzzards. Garganey (Anas querquedula), pintail (Anas acuta), black-headed ibis (Threskiornis malanocephalus), large white egret (Egretta alba modesta), cattle egret (Bubulcus ibis), whistling teal (Dendrocygna javanica), spoonbill (Platalea leucorodia), and purple heron (Ardea purpurea) are among the wetland bird species that can be observed in Wilpattu.


Reptiles:

The mugger crocodile (Crocodylus palustris), common cobra (Naja naja), Indian python (Python molurus), rat snake (Ptyas mucosus), pond turtle (Melanonchelys trijuga), and soft shelled turtle (Lissemys punctata) are the most common reptiles found in the park. All of these species reside in the large permanent Villus.
